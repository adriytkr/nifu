## Introduction

The **Mean Value Theorem** is easy to understand and essential to prove **powerful statements** such as

- [The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus)
- [Taylor's Theorem with Remainder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor%27s_theorem)
- [Corollaries](#corollaries)

The Mean Value Thereom (or MVT) is very straightforward. It basically states that at some point on a given interval, **the derivative equals the average rate of change**.

MVT can be formally stated as follow

<TheoremBox>
  **Mean Value Theorem** Let $f$ be a function continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$. Then, there exists some $c \in (a,b)$ suc that

  $$
    f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}
  $$
</TheoremBox>

Since the derivative can be represented as a tangent line and the average rate of change can be seen as the secant line, geometrically, **the secant line is parallel to the tangent line** at some point.

<ImageWithCaption>
  Tangent line and secant line have the same slope
</ImageWithCaption>

While this parallel relationship looks purely geometrical, it has profound implications for how we measure things.

## Formal proof of the Mean Value Theorem

To prove the Mean Value Theorem, we first look at a special case where the start and end points are at the same height. This is known as **Rolle's Theorem**.

### Rolle's Theorem (Special case)

Rolle's Theorem is a special case of the Mean Value Theorem.

It describes a scenario where the endpoints of the interval share the same height $f(a)=f(b)$. In this case, the secant line connecting the two points is perfectly horizontal.

And just like the MVT, there must be at least one point $c$ where the tangent is parallel to this secant. In this special scenario, the derivative is zero: $f'(c)=0$.

<TheoremBox>
  **Rolle's Theorem** Let $f$ be a function continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$ with $f(a)=f(b)$. Then, there exists a point $c \in (a,b)$ such that

  $$
    f'(c)=0
  $$
</TheoremBox>

#### Proof of Rolle's Theorem

Let $f$ be a function continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$ with $f(a)=f(b)$.

We can divide the proof into two cases.

**Case 1** $f$ is constant

Then we have $f'(x)=0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$. Hence, any value on $(a,b)$ can serve as $c$. So it follows that $f'(c)=0$.

**Case 2** $f$ is not constant

Since $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, the Extreme Value Theorem guarantees that $f$ attains both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum value on the interval.

Because $f$ is not constant and $f(a)=f(b)$, at least one of these extreme values (either the maximum or the minimum) must occur at a point $c$ on $(a,b)$.

Since $f$ is differentiable on $(a,b)$ and has a local extremum there, according to Fermat's Theorem, we have

$$
f'(c)=0
$$

This completes the proof.

### General version

Let $f$ be a function continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$. The function corresponding to the secant line can be written as

$$
L(x) = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a) + f(a)
$$

Our goal is to flatten our problem so that we can apply Rolle's Theorem. We do so by defining

$$
h(x)=f(x)-L(x)
$$

Notice that

$$
\begin{aligned}
h(a) &= f(a) - L(a) \\
&= f(a) - \left(\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(a-a) + f(a)\right) \\
&= f(a) - f(a) \\
&= 0
\end{aligned}
$$

Similarly

$$
\begin{aligned}
h(b) &= f(b) - L(b) \\
&= f(b) - \left(\frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}(b - a) + f(a)\right) \\
&= f(b) - \left(f(b) - f(a) + f(a)\right) \\
&= f(b) - f(b) \\
&= 0
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $h(a)=h(b)$. Since, $h$ was defined as the difference between two continuous and differentiable functions, $h$ is continuous and differentiable on $[a,b]$ and $(a,b)$ respectively. Therefore, we can apply Rolle's Theorem.

$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(x) &= f'(x) - L'(x) \\
&= f'(x) - \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}
\end{aligned}
$$

There exists $c \in (a,b)$ such that $h'(c)=0$. So it follows that

$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(c) &= f'(c) - \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} \\
&= 0
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
f'(c) - \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} = 0
$$

$$
f'(c) = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}
$$

This is the standard proof you usually see on Calculus books and I think it is great.

## Corollaries

Now, I state some useful theorems that are results of the Mean Value Theorem.

### Corollary 1: Constant Function

<TheoremBox>
**Corollary 1** If $f'(x) = 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is constant on $(a,b)$. That is, there exists $C \in (a,b)$ such that

$$
f(x)=C
$$

for all $x \in (a,b)$.
</TheoremBox>

#### Proof of Corollary 1

Let $f$ be a function such that $f'(x)=0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$. Then, $f$ is continuous on $(a,b)$ and we can apply the Mean Value Theorem on (a,b). Let $x \in (a,b)$. Then

$$
f'(x) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} = 0
$$

$$
f(b) - f(a) = 0
$$

$$
f(b) = f(a) = C
$$

for some constant C and for all $x \in (a,b)$.

### Corollay 2: Increasing/Decreasing Test

<TheoremBox>
**Corollary 2** If $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is strictly increasing on $[a,b]$.

If $f'(x) < 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is strictly decreasing on $[a,b]$.
</TheoremBox>

#### Proof of Corollary 2

We are going to prove the first half; the other half is proven similar.

Let $f$ be a function such that $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$. Then $f$ is differentiable on $(a,b)$ and we can apply Rolle's Theorem.

Let $x_1,x_2 \in (a,b)$ with $x_1 < x_2$. Then we can apply MVT on $(x_1,x_2)$

$$
\frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1} = f'(c)
$$

for some $c \in (x_1,x_2)$. Since $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, and $c \in (a,b)$, then $f'(c) > 0$ and

$$
\frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1} > 0
$$

Since $x_1 < x_2$, then $x_2 - x_1 > 0$. So, for the above equation to be true, we need $f(x_2) - f(x_1) > 0$. So

$$
f(x_2) > f(x_1)
$$

And by definition, $f$ is strictly increasing on $(a,b)$.

### Corollary 3: Indefinite integrals

<TheoremBox>
**Corollary 3** If $f'(x) = g'(x)$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ and $g$ differ by a constant. That is, $f(x) = g(x) + C$ for some constant C.
</TheoremBox>

#### Proof of corollay 3

Let $f$ and $g$ be functions such that $f'(x) = g'(x)$ for all $x \in (a,b)$.

Let $h(x) = f(x) - g(x)$. Then, $h'(x) = f'(x) - g'(x) = 0$.

Since $h'(x) = 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, by corollary 1, $h(x) = C$ for some constant C.

So

$$
\begin{aligned}
h(x) &= f(x) - g(x) \\
&= C
\end{aligned}
$$

Then

$$
f(x) = g(x) + C
$$

**Attention** This is why you have +C in the indefinite integrals
