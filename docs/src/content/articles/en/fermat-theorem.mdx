## Pre-requisites

To fully understand Fermat's Theorem, I merely recommend a basic familiarity with derivatives.

## Introduction

Fermat's Theorem is a very important theorem in optimization problems. It gives a very straightforward method to calculate maxima and minima of most functions.

<TheoremBox>
  If $f$ has the following properties:
  
  - $f$ has a local maximum or local minium at $c$.
  - $f$ is differentiable at $c$.

  Then,

  $$
    f'(c) = 0
  $$
</TheoremBox>

We can understand it visually

<ImageWithCaption>
  The derivative at the maximum is zero, that is, the tangent line is horizontal
</ImageWithCaption>

## Proof

Let $f$ be a function differentiable at $c$, and, without loss of generality, assume $f$ has a local maximum at $c$ (the other part is prove similarly).

Since $f$ is differentiable at $c$, then

$$
\lim_{x \to c} \frac{f(x) - f(c)}{x - c}
$$

exists.

Notice, since $f$ has a local maximum at $c$, we have $f(x) \le f(c)$ for all $x$ near $c$, or $f(x) - f(c) \le 0$.

Now, we take the one-sided limits.

**Case 1** Left-hand sided limit

Then, $x - c < 0$ and we have

$$
\lim_{x \to c^-} \frac{f(x) - f(c)}{x - c} \ge 0
$$

Similarly, for the right-hand sided limit, we have $x - c > 0$ and hence

$$
\lim_{x \to c^+} \frac{f(x) - f(c)}{x - c} \le 0
$$

For both equations to be true, we need

$$
\lim_{x \to c} \frac{f(x) - f(c)}{x - c} = 0
$$

or, $f'(c)=0$.
